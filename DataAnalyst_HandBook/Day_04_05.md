# 📌 Concept 1 : Think Before Writing SQL

## My Understanding

I learned that SQL interview questions should never be solved by immediately thinking about SQL functions.

Instead, I should first understand the business problem and identify what transformation the data needs to go through.

The framework I should follow is:

Business Requirement
        ↓
Understand the required metric
        ↓
Identify the required data
        ↓
Choose the SQL strategy
        ↓
Write SQL
        ↓
Optimize

The SQL function is the result of the thinking process, not the starting point.

---

## Why Is This Important?

Most candidates know SQL syntax.

Interviewers evaluate whether I can understand business requirements, choose the correct approach and explain my reasoning.

A candidate who communicates their thinking clearly usually performs better than someone who immediately starts writing SQL.

---

## Real World Example

Business Requirement:

Find customers who have never placed an order.

Thinking Process:

Requirement

Return customers who do not have any matching orders.

Observation

I need to compare the Customers table with the Orders table.

Approach

Find customers whose CustomerID does not exist in the Orders table.

Chosen Strategy

Use `NOT EXISTS` because the business requirement is to return customers without matching records.

---

## Interview Question

Q. Why did you choose `NOT EXISTS` instead of `LEFT JOIN`?

### My Answer

I chose `NOT EXISTS` because it directly represents the business requirement of finding customers without matching orders.

`LEFT JOIN ... IS NULL` is also a valid solution, but `NOT EXISTS` clearly expresses the intention and avoids potential issues that can occur with `NOT IN` when NULL values are present.

# 📌 Concept 2 : Data Flow Thinking

## My Understanding

Instead of asking:

"Which SQL function should I use?"

I should ask:

"What transformations should the data go through?"

Every SQL problem can be broken into logical steps before writing any code.

---

## Why Is This Important?

When the data flow is correct, the SQL becomes much easier to write.

This approach also works in SQL, Pandas, Power BI and ETL pipelines.

---

## Real World Example

Business Requirement:

Find customers whose total spending is greater than the average spending of all customers.

Data Flow

      Orders
        ↓
Calculate Total Spending per Customer
        ↓
Calculate Average Customer Spending
        ↓
Compare Customer Total with Average
        ↓
Return Matching Customers

---

## Interview Question

Q. Why shouldn't we directly calculate `AVG(amount)` from the Orders table?

### My Answer

Because the business asks for the average spending of customers, not the average value of individual orders.

First, total spending must be calculated for each customer.

Only then should the average of those customer totals be calculated.

Otherwise, the business metric changes and the answer becomes incorrect.

---

# 💡 Biggest Learning

The hardest part of SQL is not writing the query.

The hardest part is correctly understanding the business requirement and defining the business metric before choosing the SQL approach.

---

## ❌ Common Mistakes

• Jumping directly to SQL functions before understanding the problem.

• Confusing Average Order Amount with Average Customer Spending.

• Choosing SQL functions based on memory instead of business requirements.

• Explaining SQL syntax instead of explaining business reasoning during interviews.

---

## 🌍 Real-Life Application

Whenever I receive a SQL interview question or a business request, I will first draw the data flow before writing SQL.

Instead of asking:

"What function should I use?"

I will ask:

"What transformations does the data need to go through?"

---

## 📚 Lesson Learned

A good Data Analyst doesn't think in SQL.

A good Data Analyst thinks in **data transformations**, and SQL is simply the tool used to implement them.
